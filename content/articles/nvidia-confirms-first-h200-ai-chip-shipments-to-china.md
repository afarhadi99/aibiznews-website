# Nvidia Confirms First H200 AI Chip Shipments to China Since Export Curbs

## The Lead

Jeffrey Kessler doesn't usually make market-moving statements by accident, but on July 14 he made one anyway. Testifying before the House Foreign Affairs Committee at a hearing on the Bureau of Industry and Security's fiscal 2027 budget, the Commerce Department's Under Secretary for Industry and Security told lawmakers that Nvidia has, in fact, started shipping H200 AI chips to China under the license regime the Trump administration built at the end of last year. "For the American people, the bottom line is very few shipments against licenses for H200s and equivalents have taken place," Kessler said. Pressed further, he was blunter: "Very small quantity of chips, so it's trivial." Nvidia shares still rose 4.06% that day to close at $211.80, pushing the company's market capitalization to roughly $5.13 trillion, on confirmation that the pipe — however narrow — is finally open.

## The Nut Graf

The headline fact is simple: after a year of promises, revenue-share deals, and congressional hearings, actual Nvidia silicon rated for frontier AI training is moving into China again, and a U.S. government official has said so under oath. But the more important fact is the qualifier attached to it. Kessler confirmed roughly ten major Chinese companies — named across the reporting as Alibaba, Tencent, ByteDance, and a ZTE Corp unit, with DeepSeek separately entangled in the approval process on both the U.S. and Chinese sides — have been cleared to buy H200s. Yet the actual volume moving is, in Kessler's word, "trivial." This is a story about a door opening a crack, not a floodgate.

## What Actually Changed

The policy shift traces to December 2025, when the Trump administration announced it would allow Nvidia to sell H200 chips — a Hopper-generation part one rung below Nvidia's current Blackwell architecture — to vetted Chinese buyers, conditioned on a 25% cut of sales revenue flowing to the U.S. government and on national-security certifications for each purchasing company, including on-site compliance inspections. On January 13, 2026, the Bureau of Industry and Security formalized the mechanics, replacing the Biden-era "presumption of denial" standard for advanced chip exports to China with case-by-case licensing review. By late January and into February, the Commerce Department had cleared approximately ten Chinese firms, with Alibaba reportedly approved to buy hundreds of thousands of units in an order valued near $10 billion. Tencent and ByteDance were named in the same batch; a ZTE Corp subsidiary and two additional firms picked up approvals more recently, according to Reuters reporting carried by outlets including BusinessWorld and the South China Morning Post.

What hadn't happened, until Kessler's testimony, was confirmation that any chips had actually left the loading dock. Reuters and CNBC had both reported as recently as this spring that licenses existed on paper while shipments sat at zero. Kessler's July 14 remarks are the first on-the-record acknowledgment that real, physical shipments have begun — even as he declined to specify which companies received chips, how many units moved, or when the first shipment occurred.

## The Business Stakes

For Nvidia, the dollar stakes are large in theory and negligible in the current quarter. China was once worth $10 billion-plus in annual data-center revenue to the company before the export-control era began tightening in 2022 and 2023. Since then, CEO Jensen Huang has told investors, most recently in a May 2026 CNBC interview, to "expect nothing" from China in Nvidia's official guidance, and the company has zeroed out anticipated Chinese AI-chip revenue from its financial projections since 2025. That framing matters now: any H200 revenue that does materialize from China is upside Nvidia isn't modeling, not a hole it needs to fill. A Nvidia spokesperson declined to comment on Kessler's testimony.

For the Chinese buyers, the calculus is different. Alibaba, Tencent, and ByteDance are all racing to train and serve large language models against Chinese domestic accelerators from Huawei and others that remain behind Nvidia's Hopper-generation silicon in raw performance and, more critically, in the CUDA software ecosystem their engineering teams already use. Even a "trivial" number of H200s buys these firms continuity — the ability to keep existing training pipelines running on familiar tooling — more than it buys them a step-change in capability, since Nvidia's most advanced Blackwell chips remain fully banned from Chinese export under the current framework. The pent-up appetite is real regardless of how the politics resolve: separate South China Morning Post reporting, citing people familiar with the matter, says Chinese technology companies have already placed orders for more than two million H200 units for 2026, against a Nvidia inventory of roughly 700,000 units, prompting Nvidia to ask TSMC to expand production. That gap between demand and what's actually shipping is the real story sitting underneath Kessler's "trivial" — Beijing itself has not yet cleared the bulk of those orders to move, even where Washington has approved the license.

## The Evidence

The clearest evidence is Kessler's own testimony, delivered under questioning at a hearing centered on BIS's request to roughly double its enforcement budget for fiscal 2027. Kessler was pressed by committee members from both parties. Rep. Gregory Meeks of New York, the panel's ranking Democrat, noted that the Commerce Department has not added a single Chinese entity to its export-control blacklist since October — the longest such gap in more than a decade — and characterized the administration's approach as treating export licenses as "a bargaining chip" in broader trade negotiations with Beijing. Rep. Bill Huizenga of Michigan pushed Kessler on a May 31 BIS guidance document that critics say opened a loophole allowing subsidiaries of Chinese companies located outside China to obtain Blackwell chips, and asked pointedly why that guidance suggested firms could keep Blackwell chips they'd already obtained "through either smuggling or other loopholes." Rep. Young Kim raised separate concerns about foreign-foundry subsidiary workarounds and whether U.S. chipmaking-equipment controls are adequately aligned with allied restrictions in Japan and the Netherlands.

Kessler also disclosed that the Commerce Department has held off adding DeepSeek, memory-chip maker ChangXin Memory Technologies, and more than 100 other flagged firms to the Entity List — the roster that would cut off their access to U.S. technology entirely — even though a State Department official has alleged DeepSeek supported Chinese military and intelligence work and used Southeast Asian shell companies to illegally obtain restricted Nvidia chips. Kessler's explanation, in effect, was that the administration is trying to avoid escalating tensions with Beijing while the broader trade relationship is still being negotiated. He added that "there will be future regulatory action in the area of chips and AI," without elaborating.

## The Competitive Read

DeepSeek's presence in this story is a useful illustration of how tangled the approval chain has become. On the American side, Washington has so far declined to blacklist DeepSeek despite the espionage-adjacent allegations aired at the hearing. On the Chinese side, separate reporting has indicated Beijing's own regulators have been weighing whether to let Alibaba, ByteDance, and DeepSeek buy H200s at all, reportedly capping combined purchases across the group at under 200,000 units and restricting their use strictly to AI model training — a reminder that China's government, wary of dependence on foreign silicon it might lose access to again, is not simply waving Nvidia chips through even when Washington allows the sale. Both governments are, in effect, running their own gate on the same handful of companies, and Kessler's testimony captured only the U.S. side of that gate creaking open.

## The Operator Read

For a chip-buying enterprise or cloud operator watching this from the outside, the practical takeaway is that "H200 available in China" is not the same as "H200 available to your Chinese subsidiary or vendor next quarter." The licensing process still requires each buyer to clear national-security review and submit to inspections, the 25% revenue surcharge raises effective landed cost, and the total volume so far is, by the U.S. government's own description, trivial. Procurement teams and outside analysts, including KeyBanc — which reiterated an Overweight rating on Nvidia and raised its price target to $330 from $310 around this news — are reading the shipment confirmation as a signal about direction of travel rather than a near-term revenue driver. Nvidia's own operational focus for the next several quarters is reportedly weighted toward shipping B300 (Blackwell Ultra) GPUs rather than next-generation Rubin-architecture parts, which are running into production delays — underscoring that China licensing is a peripheral story to Nvidia's core supply-and-demand problem in the U.S. and allied markets.

## The Risk

The risk running through this story is political, not commercial. Bipartisan committee members used the hearing to argue that the administration has weakened the enforcement architecture around chip exports at the same moment it's approving more of them — the Entity List freeze since October, the Blackwell subsidiary loophole Huizenga raised, and the unresolved status of DeepSeek all point to the same underlying tension between an administration trying to keep trade talks with Beijing on track and a bipartisan national-security bloc in Congress that thinks the guardrails are eroding. If that political pressure builds, the license regime that let the first H200s ship could just as easily tighten again — Kessler's own comment that "there will be future regulatory action" cuts in either direction.

## What To Watch Next

Watch for the Commerce Department to disclose more specifics — or be forced to by Congress — on which of the roughly ten cleared firms have actually received chips and in what quantity, since Kessler declined to specify either. Watch whether Beijing's own reported cap on combined Alibaba-ByteDance-DeepSeek purchases (under 200,000 units, restricted to training use) becomes public policy or stays informal guidance. Watch the Entity List for whether DeepSeek or ChangXin Memory Technologies get added, which would override any Commerce-side H200 license. And watch Nvidia's next earnings call for whether the company continues to guide China revenue at zero, or begins quantifying what "trivial" shipments are worth.
