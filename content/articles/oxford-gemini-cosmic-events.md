## Introduction

Oxford and Google researchers showed that Gemini can classify astronomical transients with roughly 93% accuracy using just 15 examples per survey - producing explanations for each decision. The result points to explainable, few-shot AI assistants for science at large.

Astronomers just taught a general-purpose AI to spot exploding stars - with only a handful of examples. In a study published October 8, 2025, Oxford researchers and Google Cloud used Gemini to classify transient sky events with about 93% accuracy using just 15 annotated images per survey.

Here's how it works: Gemini receives three small images per alert - new, reference, and difference - and returns a real-vs-artifact judgment, a plain-English explanation, and an "interest score" for follow-up. That transparency helps scientists catch mistakes and trust what the AI sees.

This few-shot approach means more science with less labeling, and faster triage across thousands of nightly alerts. Instead of bespoke models trained on millions of examples, researchers can adapt a frontier model with clear instructions and a tiny dataset.

For astronomy, that's a force multiplier. For science broadly, it's a template: AI assistants that reason, explain, and accelerate discovery without massive data or ML expertise.
