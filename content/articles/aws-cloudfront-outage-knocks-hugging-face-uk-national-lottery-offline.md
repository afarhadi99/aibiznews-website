# AWS CloudFront Outage Knocks Hugging Face, UK National Lottery Offline for Hours

## The Lead

At 1:45am Pacific time on July 16, a routing subsystem inside Amazon Web Services' CloudFront content delivery network stopped loading updated configuration data correctly. Within minutes, sites and apps that had nothing in common except a shared piece of AWS plumbing began throwing 5xx server errors: the AI model-hosting platform Hugging Face, the UK's National Lottery, Bethesda's online game Fallout 76, the crypto lending protocol Morpho, and a cluster of Japanese consumer services including Hatena Blog, Niconico Video, the government's My Number digital-ID portal, and the payments app PayPay. Users trying to load any of them got a version of the same message: "We can't connect to the server for this app or website at this time. There might be too much traffic or a configuration error." AWS engineers spent roughly three and a half hours diagnosing and mitigating the fault before declaring it resolved, with a fuller root-cause explanation following later that morning.

## The Nut Graf

The outage was narrower than it looked from the outside. It hit only CloudFront customers using a feature called VPC Origins — a security-oriented option, introduced at re:Invent in late 2024, that lets a company route CloudFront traffic to private load balancers and EC2 instances without exposing them to the public internet. Everyone else on CloudFront was untouched. But because VPC Origins sits behind a shared fleet of network processors, a single internal capacity limit inside that fleet was enough to take down an AI infrastructure company, a national gambling operator, a video game, a DeFi lending protocol with billions in deposits, and government login portals simultaneously — a vivid, low-cost demonstration of how much of the internet's plumbing now runs through one company's control plane.

## What Actually Changed

According to AWS's own account, the fault traced back to "an internal constraint on the fleet managing connections to private VPC origins." When that fleet hit its limit, the system responsible for pushing updated routing configuration out to CloudFront's network processors failed to load the new data correctly, so requests bound for customers' VPC-hosted origins couldn't be routed properly. AWS's status dashboard described it plainly: "We are experiencing increased 5xx errors for CloudFront customers utilizing VPC Origins connectivity," adding that "customers utilizing other origin types remain unaffected by this issue."

The timeline ran roughly as follows: the error spike began at 12:45–1:45am PDT (accounts differ by an hour, but AWS's own incident record anchors the start at 0145 PDT, 0945 UTC); AWS Support acknowledged the investigation publicly on X around 1:57am PDT; the company posted its root-cause assessment near 3:18am PDT (11:18 UTC); and elevated errors persisted until roughly 4:18am PDT before AWS began a phased mitigation, testing the fix before rolling it out broadly. A more complete explanation followed later in the morning. Throughout, AWS's recommended workaround was blunt: customers who didn't strictly need the private-origin architecture could temporarily switch their CloudFront distributions back to public origin types to route around the broken subsystem, then "safely revert" once AWS confirmed the fix had landed.

The irony is that VPC Origins exists specifically to make applications more secure by hiding backend servers from the public internet entirely — no exposed IP addresses, no direct route around CloudFront. AWS markets it at no additional cost as a way to shrink a company's attack surface. On July 16, the customers who had adopted that best practice were precisely the ones who went dark, while anyone still routing through old-fashioned public origins sailed through untouched.

## The Business Stakes

AWS is not a peripheral player being tested by a niche feature bug — it is the piece of infrastructure roughly a third of the internet's back-end runs on, and CloudFront is its edge network for actually delivering that content to users. Hugging Face has become foundational infrastructure in its own right: enterprises and independent developers pull models, datasets, and inference endpoints from it constantly, so hours of unavailability "from most regions in the world," as the company itself described it, rippled into every downstream product quietly depending on Hugging Face staying up. The UK National Lottery is a regulated gambling operator processing millions of pounds in ticket sales and instant-win transactions daily; when it confirmed on X that players couldn't reach its website or app because of "a wider AWS outage," it wasn't just an inconvenience — it was hours of lost regulated-gambling revenue and a public admission that its resilience depends entirely on a single cloud vendor's internal fleet capacity. Morpho, the second-largest onchain lending protocol, had just raised $175 million in June on the strength of roughly $11 billion in deposits; its web app and API at app.morpho.org and api.morpho.org went dark even though, as principal engineer Julien Thomas noted once service returned, "the underlying blockchain contracts remained unaffected" — a reminder that even decentralized-finance products routinely centralize their front doors on AWS. In Japan, the outage reached into government services: the My Number Portal, the digital identity system citizens use to file taxes and access benefits, reported login failures, alongside consumer disruptions at Hatena Blog, Niconico, and the payments app PayPay.

## The Evidence

The paper trail here is unusually well documented because AWS narrated the incident in near-real time on its Service Health Dashboard and on X, and because affected customers narrated it right back. AWS's own language — "increased 5xx errors," "customers utilizing other origin types remain unaffected" — pinpointed the blast radius precisely to VPC Origins users. Hugging Face's status acknowledgment that its platform was unavailable "from most regions in the world" and the National Lottery's public admission on X that its site and app were unreachable "due to a wider AWS outage" both came directly from the affected companies, not from speculation. Downdetector logged more than 350 user reports flagging AWS problems during the incident window — a modest number by the standard of AWS's last major failure, the October 2025 US-EAST-1 disruption that generated 6.5 million reports and touched more than 1,000 companies including Snapchat, Fortnite, Roblox, and McDonald's, but still enough to put "CloudFront" and "AWS down" trending within the hour. When one frustrated customer pressed AWS Support on X for answers, the reply captured the mood of the whole morning: "I can understand the frustration, and want to assure you our team is actively working on this."

## The Competitive Read

Every AWS outage since October has reopened the same argument about concentration risk in cloud infrastructure, and this one is no exception. Mayur Upadhyaya, CEO of the monitoring firm APIContext, called it part of a "concerning trend" in provider consolidation, warning that "a fault that might once have affected a handful of organizations can now impact thousands simultaneously." That's a real dynamic, not just a talking point: AWS controls roughly the largest share of global cloud infrastructure spend, ahead of Microsoft Azure and Google Cloud, and posted accelerating growth in its most recent quarterly results as AI workloads pile onto the platform. CloudFront itself competes in a CDN market that includes Cloudflare and Akamai, both of which stayed up while CloudFront's VPC Origins customers went dark — a fact rivals will not be shy about repeating to prospective customers evaluating multi-CDN strategies. But the deeper competitive question is about lock-in rather than market share: Hugging Face, the National Lottery, and Morpho didn't choose CloudFront in isolation — they chose it as part of a broader AWS commitment that's expensive and slow to unwind, which is exactly why repeat incidents keep landing on the same well-known names rather than forcing rapid vendor diversification.

## The Operator Read

For engineering teams, the uncomfortable lesson is that the "secure" architecture choice became the single point of failure. VPC Origins was adopted precisely to remove public internet exposure — and AWS's own emergency guidance during the incident was to undo that hardening and route back through public origin types just to restore service, which only worked for customers who'd kept a fallback path available at all. Teams that had fully committed to private-origin-only architectures, with no public origin configured as a break-glass option, had nothing to fall back on and simply had to wait out AWS's fix. The practical takeaway circulating among site-reliability teams by midday was straightforward: treat any single managed feature — however security-forward — as a potential outage vector, keep an alternate origin path configured and tested even if you never intend to use it in normal operation, and don't assume a feature's narrow customer base makes it a narrow risk when that customer base includes AI infrastructure, gambling operators, and government ID portals at the same time.

## The Risk

The recurrence risk is the headline concern: this is the second time in roughly nine months that a single AWS subsystem fault has cascaded into a multi-industry, multi-continent outage, following October 2025's US-EAST-1 incident. Each event is individually explainable — a DNS race condition then, a fleet capacity constraint in a routing-configuration pipeline now — but the pattern points to fragility in the control-plane layers that sit underneath supposedly redundant, regionally distributed services. There's regulatory exposure too: UK financial and gambling regulators have flagged cloud concentration as a systemic risk before, and a regulated national lottery operator publicly blaming AWS for hours of downtime is the kind of incident that feeds directly into that policy conversation. There's also a compounding AI risk specific to this event — as more of the software economy routes model inference and hosting through platforms like Hugging Face, an AWS-level fault doesn't just take down one product, it takes down the AI supply chain underneath dozens of products that assumed that supply chain was solid.

## What To Watch Next

Expect AWS to publish a formal post-incident report — the kind of detailed "Correction of Errors" writeup it has produced after past major disruptions — addressing what specific capacity limit was hit in the VPC Origins fleet and what changes it's making to prevent a repeat. Watch whether Hugging Face, the National Lottery, and Morpho disclose any service-credit claims or their own internal postmortems, and whether AWS enterprise customers use this outage as leverage in contract renegotiations. Watch too for movement on multi-CDN and multi-cloud adoption commentary from analysts and from Cloudflare and Akamai, who have every incentive to court customers spooked by a second cascading AWS failure in under a year. And watch AWS's next earnings call for any reliability-investment talking points, given how central uptime commentary has become to the hyperscaler's pitch to enterprise and AI customers alike.
