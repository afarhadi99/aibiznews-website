# Bending Spoons' IPO Puts AOL, Vimeo, And Eventbrite Back On Wall Street

Bending Spoons is now a public company, and that turns a strange collection of internet history into a live market test. The Milan software operator priced its Nasdaq IPO at $29 a share, above the marketed range, and began trading under the ticker BSP. The company is not selling one breakout app. It is selling a machine for buying familiar digital products, rebuilding them, and turning their users into a larger subscription base.

The brands are the hook. AOL, Vimeo, Eventbrite, Evernote, WeTransfer, Komoot, Brightcove, and other acquired products sit inside a company most consumers still do not know by name. That disconnect is the point. Bending Spoons wants public investors to value the operating system behind those brands more than the individual products themselves.

The IPO terms show the scale. The final prospectus lists 57,971,015 ordinary shares at $29 each, split between 34,398,640 shares sold by Bending Spoons and 23,572,375 shares sold by existing shareholders. The document puts total gross offering value at roughly $1.68 billion, with just under $954 million in proceeds to the company before expenses and about $654 million to selling shareholders.

The market reaction was stronger than the filing price implied. Associated Press reported that BSP jumped 39.7% on its first trading day, giving the company a market value of about $25.2 billion. That is a large public endorsement for a company whose pitch sounds closer to a software private-equity platform than a conventional product startup.

The operating story is unusually specific. Bending Spoons says its playbook is to acquire digital businesses, deeply integrate them onto its platform, and rebuild technology, product, monetization, and parts of the team. In an Axios interview, co-founder and CEO Luca Ferrari said the company looks first for predictability and for businesses it believes it can improve dramatically. He also said the company is not pursuing a thematic portfolio where every asset cross-sells with every other one.

That matters because this is not simply a nostalgia trade in AOL or a recovery trade in Vimeo. Public investors are being asked to underwrite a repeatable transformation model. If Bending Spoons can keep buying mature software assets, standardize engineering and data systems, tighten pricing, and expand subscription revenue, the company could become a public-market version of an acquisition compounder. If the model stalls, it could become a heavily leveraged owner of old internet brands.

The financials cut both ways. AP reported that Bending Spoons generated $601 million in revenue and $27.5 million in net income in the first quarter of 2026, with more than 500 million monthly active users and 9 million monthly paying customers as of March. Those numbers explain why the market paid attention. The same coverage also noted debt of just under $4.4 billion, which makes continued operating improvement and disciplined acquisitions more important.

Control is another key part of the story. The prospectus says Matteo Danieli, Luca Ferrari, Francesco Patarnello, and Luca Querella will beneficially own class A shares that can exercise 82.71% of voting power immediately after the offering. That gives the operators room to run the playbook, but it also means outside shareholders are buying economics with limited governance influence.

The AI angle is operational, not cosmetic. Bending Spoons presents itself as a company that uses proprietary technology, proprietary data, and automation to make acquired products more efficient. That is more concrete than a generic AI label. The real question is whether AI tools make software consolidation structurally cheaper, or whether the hard parts of integration, retention, product trust, and customer support remain stubbornly human.

For the tech market, BSP is worth watching because it asks a timely question: are mature internet products better off as standalone companies, private-equity assets, or pieces of an AI-assisted operating platform? The first trading day gave Bending Spoons a premium valuation. The next few quarters will show whether the company can turn AOL, Vimeo, Eventbrite, and the next wave of acquisitions into durable public-company earnings rather than a clever IPO story.
