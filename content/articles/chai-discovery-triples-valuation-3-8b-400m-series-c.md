## The Lead

Seven months ago, Chai Discovery was worth $1.3 billion. On July 14, 2026, the San Francisco startup announced it had closed a $400 million Series C at a $3.8 billion valuation — nearly tripling its worth in the time it takes a Phase 1 trial to enroll its first patients. The round landed one day after Chai disclosed a new collaboration with Novartis, which meant that by the time investors' term sheets were signed, the two-year-old company counted Eli Lilly, Pfizer and Novartis as customers simultaneously. Index Ventures led the financing, with Kleiner Perkins, Sequoia Capital and Dimension returning alongside a wave of new backers including Bain Capital Ventures, Battery Ventures and Baillie Gifford. Dimension founder Zavain Dar didn't hedge about what the check meant for his own fund: "This is the largest check we've ever written at Dimension."

## The Nut Graf

Chai Discovery builds generative AI models that design antibodies and other biologic molecules from scratch, rather than screening existing chemical libraries for hits. Founded in 2024 by Joshua Meier, Jack Dent, Matthew McPartlon and Jacques Boitreaud, the company has gone from a roughly $30 million seed round to a $3.8 billion valuation in under two years, raising four rounds totaling around $630 million along the way. What makes this raise more than a paper valuation exercise is the customer list attached to it: three of the largest pharmaceutical companies in the world are already paying to use Chai's models on live drug programs, and the newest of those deals, with Novartis, was struck the day before the funding closed. That sequencing — commercial traction driving the round, not the other way around — is the story.

## What Actually Changed

The mechanics of the raise are straightforward. Chai's Series B, a $130 million round co-led by Oak HC/FT and General Catalyst in December 2025, valued the company at $1.3 billion. The new $400 million Series C, led by Index Ventures with Kleiner Perkins, Sequoia Capital and Dimension returning, pushes that to $3.8 billion — a roughly 2.9x step-up in seven months. New investors joining this round include Bain Capital Ventures, Battery Ventures, Baillie Gifford, BDT & MSD, Sapphire Ventures and Avra Capital, while earlier backers Thrive Capital, OpenAI, Oak HC/FT, Menlo Ventures, General Catalyst, Glade Brook, Avenir, Lachy Groom and Yosemite all returned. That is an unusually dense investor syndicate for a Series C, and it reflects how quickly Chai's funding history has compressed: a roughly $30 million seed from Thrive Capital, OpenAI and Dimension, a $70 million Series A led by Menlo Ventures in August 2025, the $130 million Series B in December, and now this $400 million round — four rounds in about two years.

What changed underneath the valuation is the product line and the customer roster. Chai launched its first model, Chai-1, in 2024 for multimodal biomolecular structure prediction across proteins, small molecules, DNA and RNA. Chai-2, released in 2025, was the company's first generative system for zero-shot de novo antibody design — meaning it proposes antibody sequences for a target it has never seen before, rather than optimizing existing candidates. Chai-3, the newest model referenced in the funding announcement, further improves target success rates and binding affinity; the company says it produces antibodies that bind substantially more tightly to intended targets than its predecessor did. Layered on top of the model releases are three enterprise deals: a research collaboration with Eli Lilly in January 2026 that includes a bespoke model trained on Lilly's proprietary data, a licensing agreement with Pfizer in June 2026 giving it early access to Chai-3 plus a custom model, and the Novartis collaboration on AI-driven antibody discovery announced July 13 — the day before the Series C.

## The Business Stakes

For Chai, the money buys runway to keep shipping model generations at the pace its pharma customers now expect, and to build out the commercial and scientific staff needed to service simultaneous partnerships with three top-15 global drug makers without any one of them feeling like an afterthought. For the pharmaceutical industry, the stakes are more structural. Drug development still costs roughly $2.6 billion per approved therapy and takes about a decade, and the industry is bracing for what analysts have pegged as roughly $200 billion in lost revenue from patent expirations by 2030. Antibody and biologic pipelines are exactly where companies like Lilly, Pfizer and Novartis are looking to compress that timeline, because generative design that can propose a working binder in silico — rather than requiring years of wet-lab screening — attacks the single most expensive and slowest part of early discovery.

For the venture investors, the stakes are about whether AI-native "picks and shovels" companies in biotech can command software-style multiples while selling into an industry that moves at pharma speed. CEO Joshua Meier framed the pitch in explicitly engineering terms: "Tomorrow's medicines should be designed with the precision, speed and scale of modern engineering." Index Ventures partner Nina Achadjian, who led the round, said conviction came early: "From the moment I met this team, I knew they were the ones to tackle one of medicine's hardest problems." Sequoia's Pat Grady put the commercial argument even more directly: "Chai's product velocity and real-world partnerships are turning the AI drug discovery dream into reality."

## The Evidence

The clearest evidence Chai has something pharma companies will pay for is the deal cadence itself: three named partnerships — Lilly, Pfizer, Novartis — struck within roughly six months of each other, culminating in the Novartis announcement the day before the fundraise closed. That is not typical for a company barely two years old. The model performance numbers back up the narrative, even if outlets differ slightly on the exact figures. Chai has said Chai-2 achieved double-digit experimental hit rates for zero-shot antibody design against targets it had never encountered, compared with under 1% for prior computational methods — commentators have described that jump as roughly a hundredfold improvement over legacy approaches. Chai-3, the model referenced in the announcement and now licensed to Pfizer, is described as cutting antibody design failure rates by around half again relative to Chai-2, while improving binding affinity and enabling multi-specific antibody engineering — designing a single molecule to hit more than one target.

Kleiner Perkins partner Ilya Fushman pointed to the combination of technical depth and commercial pull as the reason his firm returned for a second round: "Chai has both technical brilliance and remarkable commercial velocity." That "commercial velocity" phrase is doing real work here — it is the language of a growth-stage software round, applied to a company whose product output is antibody sequences rather than SaaS seats, and it signals how investors are now underwriting Chai less like a research lab and more like an infrastructure vendor with recurring pharma contracts.

## The Competitive Read

Chai is not operating in a vacuum, and the two most relevant comparisons show how fast the category is inflating. Isomorphic Labs, the Alphabet-backed AI drug discovery unit connected to DeepMind, closed a $2.1 billion Series B in May 2026, led by Thrive Capital with participation from MGX, Temasek and a UK sovereign AI fund — and Isomorphic's own partnership list also includes Novartis and Lilly, meaning two of Chai's three marquee pharma customers are simultaneously working with a much larger, Alphabet-affiliated rival. Xaira Therapeutics launched in 2024 with roughly $1 billion in committed capital, and Recursion Pharmaceuticals, after merging with Exscientia in a deal valued around $688 million, has raised more than $1 billion cumulatively pursuing a similar AI-first discovery thesis with its own biotech pipeline attached.

The distinction that matters is business model. Isomorphic and Recursion have moved toward owning or co-owning drug candidates, betting on milestone payments and eventual royalties from approved therapies. Chai has positioned itself differently — as, in effect, AI infrastructure for pharmaceuticals, selling model access and bespoke training rather than holding a stake in the molecules its models help design. That makes Chai's revenue more predictable and faster to book, but it also caps the upside if one of the antibodies its models touch turns into a blockbuster: Chai gets a licensing or services fee, not a royalty stream, unless deal terms say otherwise, which the companies have not disclosed.

## The Operator Read

Chai's founding team reads like a checklist of where the frontier-AI and computational-biology worlds intersect. Meier led protein language model research at Meta's FAIR lab and co-developed the ESM family of protein models before serving as chief AI officer at Absci, an AI-driven biologics company; co-founder Matthew McPartlon, who holds a computer science PhD from the University of Chicago, was previously the technical lead for de novo antibody design at that same company, Absci. Co-founder Jacques Boitreaud did graduate research in generative molecular modeling at McGill University and worked previously at Aqemia, a French AI drug-discovery firm. That lineage — two people who already built and shipped antibody-design AI at a public biotech company, now doing it again with a cleaner architecture and OpenAI as both an investor and, implicitly, a talent and technique connection — is a big part of why investors describe this as more than a research bet. It is also why the round closed as fast as it did: pharma buyers evaluating a vendor for a core discovery function want operators who have already done the job once.

## The Risk

The plainest risk sits outside Chai's control: despite roughly $20 billion having flowed into generative AI drug discovery companies industry-wide, no AI-discovered drug has yet won regulatory approval. Industry trackers count roughly 173 AI-originated drug programs now in clinical development, up from about two dozen in 2023 — a real increase, but still zero approvals to point to as proof the whole category converts lab success rates into approved medicines. Chai's own hit-rate improvements are measured in a lab, not a clinic, and antibody candidates that bind tightly in an assay can still fail on manufacturability, immunogenicity or efficacy years later in human trials — failure modes no design model has yet been proven to solve. There is also concentration risk in the customer base: three pharma relationships, however prestigious, is a thin base to have built a $3.8 billion valuation on, and losing or failing to renew any one of the Lilly, Pfizer or Novartis deals would be a visible signal to the next round of investors. Finally, Chai's own model cadence — three major releases in roughly two years — sets an expectation of continuous, compounding improvement that will be hard to sustain indefinitely, especially as better-funded rivals like Isomorphic chase the same targets with the same pharma partners.

## What To Watch Next

The most concrete near-term signal will be whether any of Chai's three pharma partnerships advance a specific antibody candidate into IND-enabling studies or a filed clinical trial — the first hard evidence that a Chai-designed molecule survives contact with biology outside the lab. Watch also for whether Chai adds a fourth major pharma name, which would blunt the concentration-risk argument, or instead deepens its existing deals (as it did by layering a Pfizer license on top of the Lilly collaboration) rather than broadening them. On the competitive side, Isomorphic Labs' next disclosed milestone, given its overlapping Novartis and Lilly relationships, will be a direct read on whether pharma companies are hedging by running two AI-design vendors against the same problem or genuinely dividing the work. And expect scrutiny of Chai-3's real-world hit rates once any of its pharma partners discuss program-level results publicly, since so far the performance numbers driving a $3.8 billion valuation come from Chai itself.
